Gender-sensitive reporting
and Intersectional Media and Information Literacy

o4

Changing the narrative around gender-sensitive reporting to reflect its significance in challenging dangerous stereotypes and adding diverse (and missing!) perspectives to the media ecosystem

Change within newsrooms, as primary exporters of culture and community engagement, has significant implications for general social awareness. When it comes to the women journalists actively pushing for more diverse representation and participation, they are (as they have almost always been, if we’re honest) powerful agents of change with the experience and knowledge to alter the current narratives impeding gender equity and media freedom. Just as information and awareness of risk can empower women online, so too can exposure to new ideas, identities and lived experiences, expand normative frameworks and increase the creation and sharing of knowledge within and between communities. What is gender-sensitive reporting, exactly, and how can it impact media and information literacy?.

A meaningful approach to holistic safety, necessitates a comprehensive perspective that takes into account the multifaceted aspects and intersecting identities of women journalists in contemporary societies.

Shared principles for holistic safety:

The three main pillars of gender-sensitive reporting:

  • cross-platform nature: different platforms and online spaces (e.g YouTube, Facebook, Gmail);
  • different forms of expression (e.g. meme or doctored image);
  • different profiles and closed groups (e.g. Reddit-channels);
  • different technologies (infecting your computer with virus, surveillance tech);
  • by anonymous individuals, cyber-mob, troll farm and party booths, state authorities;
  • from someone you know → ex-partner, colleagues;
  • can last for a few hours up to several days and months.

HOW TO: promoting a new narrative and approach to holistic safety

  • Engage with opportunities for progress, rather than just focusing on obstacles and vulnerabilities that women (and other marginalized identities) face.
  • Shift the focus from women as targets of OGBV/GD to the perpetrators, in their many forms.
  • To support targets of OGBV/GD, effective and sustainable remedies address physical, emotional, and community security.
  • Address OGBV as one method for gendering disinformation, and to highlight the layered impact on individuals, communities, and democratic processes.
  • Impactful and sustainable engagement to combat OGBV/GD must prioritise women’s experiences and expertise, and the holistic and structural obstacles that threaten women’s equal representation, participation, and safety in the media ecosystem.

HOW TO: Addressing obstacles to well-being as opportunities for building resilience

  • When it comes to engagement with platforms of oppression, consider a risk/benefit analysis: What do you get out of a platform vs. what is the potential for/lived experience of harm?
  • Prioritise physical well-being: proving to ourselves that we are worth time, attention and resources. This can manifest in so many practical ways: scheduling regular doctors visits, establishing and prioritising a sleep schedule, regular exercise and/or somatic practice to encourage health and process harmful emotions, properly feeding ourselves - even when we’re on a tight deadline or have other urgent tasks, taking time off when we feel unwell or need to prioritise care-giving responsibilities. Think about it: What is a self-care for you? How do you practice it?
  • Take time and space away from work - physically (taking a walk, working from a different location, silencing work-related phone calls and meetings during private time) and emotionally (consuming content for pleasure or personal interest, establishing spaces and/ times when engagement with work is off-limits).
  • Expand your experiences and indulge curiosity - Engage with content and activities that are new to you: podcasts, cultural events, and finding new forms for personal expression through activities like dance, art, or learning a new skill.
  • Build and lean-on community: Spending quality time with friends and family - outside of care-giving responsibilities - can help recharge and restore our resilience. Get inspired and learn from others: Bloom - a healing journey course. The mutual sharing of experiences, empathy and solutions with a supportive community can be a powerful outlet for negative emotions and stress.

The Gender and Media pilot project was carried out under the auspices of the Balkan Media Assistance Program to Foster Organizational Readiness While Advancing Resilient Development (BMAP Forward), funded by USAID and implemented by Internews as part of the PROGRESS Human Rights Support Mechanism. The pilot project was established to address the nuanced and complex obstacles to participation, representation, and safety that women journalists working in Balkans media face. To address these complex issues, we employed a holistic set of tools, participatory methodology and intersectional lens to investigate and rethink existing narratives about women’s experiences in media, and the perception of women’s role as journalists and media workers - locally, regionally, and from a broader global perspective.
